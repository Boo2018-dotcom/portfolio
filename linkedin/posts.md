## Post 2 — Design Decision (single image)
**Attach:** Screenshot of post2-decision.html
**Post at:** Thursday 8:30 AM IST

```
One decision that made my Wear OS UPI design 10× better:

I deleted the UPI ID input field entirely.

UPI IDs look like this: firstname.lastname@bankname

Typing that on a 390px circular watch screen? Nearly impossible.

But user research revealed something obvious in hindsight:

Most people pay the same 5–10 contacts repeatedly.

So instead of typing, I built saved contacts that sync from the phone app once. Every repeat payment after that? One tap.

The result: Zero typing for ~90% of payment scenarios.

The real lesson: "Remove it" is almost always the right call. The best design decision I made on this project wasn't adding something — it was deleting something.

What's a feature you deleted that made your product better?

#UXDesign #ProductDesign #Fintech
```

## Post 3 — Contrarian Take (single image)
**Attach:** Screenshot of post3-contrarian.html
**Post at:** Tuesday 8:30 AM IST

```
Hot take: The worst thing you can do to a watch UI is make it look like a phone UI.

When I started designing UPI payments for Wear OS, I made this exact mistake.

I took the phone flow and "simplified" it.

Result: Still broken, just with smaller text.

The breakthrough came when I stopped thinking "small phone" and started thinking "physical trigger."

Watches are:
→ Worn, not held
→ Glanced at, not stared at
→ One-handed by default
→ Interruptible, not focused sessions

Every design decision shifted from "options" to "confidence."

One tap to confirm. Not five steps to complete.

What's a design pattern you've had to completely unlearn when moving to a new form factor?

#ProductDesign #WearOS #UX
```

## Post 4 — Constraints as Clarity (single image)
**Attach:** Screenshot of post4-constraints.html
**Post at:** Thursday 8:30 AM IST

```
"Designing within constraints is just... designing."

I didn't fully believe this until I had to fit a complete UPI payment flow into 390px.

You can't hide bad decisions on a tiny screen.

Every element I added had to justify its existence.

What the circular display taught me:

→ If it's not essential, it's a distraction
→ Trust is established in the first tap, not the last
→ "What if the user wants to...?" → Remove it. They almost never do.

The cleanest interface I've ever designed came from the most constrained canvas I've ever worked on.

The constraint wasn't the enemy of good design. It was the condition for it.

#UXDesign #DesignThinking #ProductDesign
```
