The gap isn't laziness — it's a technical-UX knot. UPI requires SIM-based device binding. QR scanning dominates the UPI experience but watches have no camera. PIN entry on a tiny circular screen seems impossible. My hypothesis: the problem isn't capability — it's interaction design.

Key Decision 01

Recipient Selection Without Typing

UPI IDs are long (name@bank) and error-prone on a tiny screen. I explored four approaches:

Full keyboard✕ High error rate, too small
Voice input✕ Awkward in public, privacy
QR scan✕ No camera on watch
✓ Saved contactsMost payments go to same 5–10 people

New recipients are added via the phone companion app first. This isn't a limitation — it's intentional friction that prevents errors.

Key Decision 02

PIN & Amount Entry — The Numpad Problem

UPI requires PIN entry — unavoidable. The goal: make the numpad work well on a circular display.

Standard grid numpad✕ Corner keys cut off by bezel
Rotary crown input✕ Slow for amounts, error-prone
✓ Optimised flat numpad44px targets, centered layout

Large touch targets (44px min) · Visual feedback on tap · PIN shown as dots · Backspace accessible.

Reflections

Learning 01

Constraints force clarity

The circular display seemed limiting — it forced every element to justify its existence. Nothing survived that couldn't earn its place.

Learning 02

"Remove a feature" is often the answer

Dropping QR scanning felt like failure at first. Removing it made every remaining flow stronger and cleaner.

Learning 03

Design systems compound

Building components first added 2 days upfront. It saved a week of inconsistency fixes across 15+ screens.

If I had more time

Voice confirmation — "Pay Ravi ₹50?" — for truly hands-free context
Haptic feedback patterns for eyes-free success/failure signals mid-workout
Watch face complications for one-tap quick-pay to frequent contacts
Spending summary glance — last 3 transactions visible without PIN
